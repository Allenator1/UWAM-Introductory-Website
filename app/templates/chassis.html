Technical Module: Chassis

Moving into the technical areas, one of the main areas of the vehicle itself is the Chassis.
For those without prior vehicle/motorsport inclined knowledge, the Chassis department deals mainly with the body of the car,
which we call the monocoque, and any structural frames associated with the body. The main work that is associated with the chassis
department is the design of the body and its supporting frames on CAD (Computer Aided Design) software, sourcing materials
from our partners / external businesses, conducting composite manufacturing work in order to convert the designs to reality, and
finally extensive testing of each manufactured piece, in order to comply with the governing regulations and prove equivalency with known safety standards.

Design

The design phase typically involves the computer conceptualisation and detailing of the years intended monocoque concept, and the
supporting structures needed for the safety of the driver, and the strength to combat all of the loads and forces to be experienced
while the vehicle is in motion. The central chassis parts that need to be designed during this phase are as listed below:

  • Monocoque
  • Impact Attenuator and Nosecone
  • Front and Main Hoop and support structures
  • Subframe

Manufacturing

The main materials and resources that the chassis team utilises to undergo successful production of the vehicle body:

  • Carbon Fibre Honeycomb (Monocoque + Impact Attenuator)
  • Epoxy (Monocoque + Nosecone + Impact Attenuator)
  • Carbon Fibre fabric (Monocoque + Nosecone)
  • Metal Sheet (Mild Steel + Aluminium)
  • Metal Tubing (Chromoly Steel)

With all the materials and resources acquired, the manufacturing stages can begin. The main involvement from new members,
in the chassis department, is directly with the manufacturing of the monocoque, which is the compartment housing the driver.
This is the most labour intensive task, and there is no prior knowledge or experience needed to undertake these jobs.
The usual process at which the monocoque is manufactured commences with the team receiving the aluminium honeycomb, and folding
the flat panel into the monocoque shape. With the shape bolted into its joints, composite work may commence, where we line the edges
with epoxy and layup with the carbon fibre fabric. We then harden the epoxy and carbon fibre, which results in stiff and strong bends;
typical of what should be expected in an FSAE type monocoque. The steel and aluminium structures, are processed through notching and
cutting of the tubes and metal sheets, formed to the shapes they need to be in through means of basic metal working processes; laser cutting,
grinding, bending, sanding, and then welded together to create the structures needed. Finally, once these elements have been completed,
they are all attached to the car through means of nuts and bolts! Note: Before the whole chassis is manufactured, the team undergoes
manufacturing of various test pieces that we can test to prove structural and safety equivalency, see ‘Testing’ for more information.

Testing

In the typical chassis manufacturing process, there are additional sample pieces made, in order to test the structural
equivalence of each component to a standard which complies to the safety regulations determined by the governing body.
These tests are conducted on various hydraulic type force actuators, and then measuring the damage associated with the various
loads and forces. Once these results are extracted, they can be cross-referenced to the safety regulations. Once properly analysed,
they can be concluded to either comply with the safety standards, in which case we can continued with the entire manufacturing of the
chassis, or fail, in which case we need to go back to the drawing board and hypothesise where we are lacking, and conduct different
strategies we can use to increase the strength and durability of components so that they lie well within the safety standards.
