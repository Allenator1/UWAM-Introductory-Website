Technical Module: Vehicle Dynamics

This area is involved in taking the power output by the motor, and efficiently transmitting it to the ground,
and utilising the resultant forces at the tyre surfaces to propel the car in whichever direction the driver wishes to go.
The main subsystems that the vehicle dynamics department is in charge of are the:

  • Suspension system
  • Steering
  • Brakes and Accelerator
  • Wheel and Tyre Assemblies

Design

The design of these systems have somewhat of a greater degree of difficulty associated with them, as the designs
will have to consider the vehicle moving under different conditions. Such conditions include heavy accelerations,
heavy braking, tight cornering. All these conditions will pinpoint the extremities of different tyre positions,
and all conditions will have to be thoroughly analysed in order to ensure that no components will be under the risk
of clashing through the cars states of motion. In addition, design consideration will have to be taken when configuring
different components, such as suspension stiffness, steering behaviour, and different vehicle dynamics parameters.
All these configurations are implemented in order to maximise the tractive potential of our race car, and hence maximise
efficiency of output power from the powertrain system.

Manufacturing

The manufacturing of the vehicle dynamics system is relatively simple. Although the parts involved with the area of the car
are extremely complex, most of the parts are outsourced from external suppliers / businesses. This is mainly done to reduce
the span of the manufacturing process, so that more time can be spent in testing the vehicle and fine tuning all the vehicle
dynamics parameters. The main outsourced components include:

  • Wheels
  • Tyres
  • Steering Rack and Pinion
  • Brake Calipers
  • Shock Absorbers

Once all the additional system components are manufactured, all the system can be assembled, and bolted onto the car!

Testing

Through the initial design phases, the aim with the entire system is to produce the fastest configuration of the race car, and
along with this, comes the need to model reality in a series of numbers and mathematical equations. Most of these modelling strategies
end up being 90% incorrect, as per usual efforts of teams with lack of experience, time, and money. Hence, the basic philosophy of
the vehicle dynamics department is to ‘take your best guess’ in design, fast-track the build in the manufacturing process, and do
all your learning on the track!

It is through testing do you get to find out if your numbers were correct or not. If they were correct (or were close to being correct),
it’s a big HOORAYY! Otherwise, time to get to the thinking part and start coming up with a list of hypothetical causes for the inaccuracy.
Once all the possible causes have been identified, it is simply a matter of using the car to work through each of the scenarios to see which
one gives you the magic BINGO!

This learning phase also involves the fine tuning of all the aforementioned vehicle dynamics parameters, with the aim of achieving the fastest
possible configuration of your race car! One of the most important factors in the vehicle dynamics area is the driver! Maximising your testing
time will directly correlate with maximising your driver confidence and his/her ability to consistently push the race car to its operating limits
and achieve the fastest lap times possible! In fact, it is shown that the teams that properly develop their drivers abilities are the teams that
find themselves winning the competitions! This directly supports the fact that the driver ability is one of the biggest factors of team performance
in competition dynamic events. There is a rough rule of thumb, that the time difference between a great driver and a novice driver, greatly outweighs
the time difference between a great car and a satisfactory car, driven by the same driver of decent driver ability.
